A system that uses the marketplace's real money to find which of thousands of products earn and which quietly burn budget — and reprices the catalogue so every active product turns a profit.

01 · problem

A store with ~5,500 products, one operator. For the question “is this product selling at a profit or a loss?” there was no answer for a single item: the marketplace withholds about half the price, applies its own discounts, and writes off some costs at the account level without tying them to specific products. Managing that by hand is impossible.

02 · solution

I built a system in Python that pulls actual financial transactions from the marketplace API — payouts, commissions, logistics, returns, penalties — and rolls them up into a P&L for every product sold. Items are then classified: profitable, thin, loss-making on price, loss-making on returns. For repricing it computes a “price floor” from cost using the real commission, not the stated one.

Every change goes through a dry-run first: the system shows a “before → after” plan, and only after a human confirms does it write prices back, keeping a snapshot for rollback.

03 · what was hard

Shutting it down looked like the obvious call. Instead I reconciled the calculation against actual transactions — and the picture was the opposite. The breakdown exposed systematic skews: the real commission was noticeably lower than stated, real logistics far below the maximum rates, and the analytics endpoint undercounted sales versus the financial one.

Hence the principle baked into the system: the predictive model is only for future prices, the financial result comes only from actual transactions.

The whole competitiveness assessment had been built on the wrong one. After reconciling shipments against payouts the rule became strict: each product's economics must add up to a profit on its own payout, without the marketplace's external discounts.

04 · result
  • ~5,500 products in the catalogue, 8.5 months of history rolled up into a P&L per item.
  • Hidden account-level leaks that were eating the entire product-level profit were found and switched off.
  • Dozens of products with pathological return rates (up to 87% returns on an item) were surfaced — pulled or neutralised with a prohibitive price.
  • 1,561 products repriced in a single day; the largest write was 1,304 prices at once, zero errors, verified by reading back.
  • No “the script took the store down” incidents: dry-run → rollback → manual gate → verification.
